# Data Analysis Project – Student Performance and Lifestyle Analysis

## Overview

This project analyzes how student lifestyle habits—particularly physical activity, phone usage, sleep, and study time—affect energy levels and concentration. The goal is to identify measurable relationships between daily behaviors and cognitive performance.

---

## Research Objective

To evaluate the impact of:

* Physical activity
* Phone usage
* Sleep duration
  on student **energy levels** and **concentration**

---

## Dataset Information

* **Sample Size:** 70 students
* **Population:** Students (school and university)
* **Sampling Method:** Convenience sampling via online survey

### Survey Questions

1. Physical activity (days per week)
2. Daily energy level (scale 1–10)
3. Study concentration (scale 1–10)
4. Sleep hours per night
5. Phone usage (hours per day)
6. Study time (hours per day)

Survey link included in report 

---

## Methodology

* Data collected through an online self-reported survey
* Quantitative analysis using descriptive statistics (mean, median, mode)
* Visualization through bar charts, histograms, and pie charts
* Focus on identifying correlations between lifestyle habits and performance

---

## Key Analysis

### 1. Energy Levels

* Mean: 5.79
* Median: 6.0
* Mode: 7.0

**Insight:**
Most students report moderate energy levels, but a few low values reduce the overall average.
(Shown in histogram on page 3)

---

### 2. Physical Activity

* Most common range: 1–2 hours per week

**Insight:**
Students generally engage in light to moderate physical activity, representing average fitness levels.

---

### 3. Phone Usage vs Concentration

* Mean concentration: 5.56
* Most common phone usage: 4–6 hours/day

**Insight:**

* Higher phone usage is associated with lower concentration levels.
* Students with the highest screen time tend to have focus scores near or below average.
  (Bar chart on page 4 illustrates this relationship)

---

### 4. Study Time Distribution

* Most common study duration: 1–2 hours/day

**Insight:**
The majority of students study for a relatively short daily duration.
(Pie chart on page 4 shows distribution across categories)

---

## Key Findings

* There is an **inverse relationship between phone usage and concentration**.
* Students maintain **moderate energy levels**, with room for improvement.
* Physical activity is present but generally limited in duration.
* Study time is relatively low for most students.

---

## Conclusion

* High phone usage appears to negatively impact concentration.

* Students could improve focus and energy by:

  * Reducing screen time
  * Increasing physical activity
  * Improving sleep habits

* A better balance of lifestyle habits could increase the average concentration level beyond the current 5.56.

---

## Limitations

* Data is self-reported, which may introduce bias
* Use of ranges (e.g., 1–2 hours) reduces precision
* Convenience sampling limits generalizability
